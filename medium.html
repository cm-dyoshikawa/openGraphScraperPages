A Brief History of Robotics

How the automotive industry bankrolled robotics development

Christian Saur
Oct 23, 2019 · 7 min read

Big thanks to Ann Kathrin Stärkel for contributing to this story!

When we first started looking into robotics, we were wondering about its history. After all, this relatively new field of technology didn’t just fall off the wagon (no pun intended). So, we came up with a rather bold hypothesis: it was the automotive industry that sparked robotics development in the early twentieth century!

A revolution — sparked by cars

The way we get around these days is merely only a product of robotics and advances in the field. However, robots played an important role in enabling today’s mobility options. Automotive companies were relying on industrial robots early on to automate their factories, cut costs and lift their quality standards. Consequentially, cars became more affordable, turning them into prime mobility options for many households. Fast forward a few decades, and robo-taxis or autonomous drones are two prominent examples of robotics at the center of how we imagine tomorrow’s mobility landscape.

With such a strong link between mobility, logistics and robotics, it is due time to take a closer look at the two spaces and how they overlap, intersect and connect. Read below a brief overview over the history of robotics in mobility.

Automation — the quest for efficiency

Detroit, 1913: By rearranging production inside his automotive plant, Henry Ford sparked a revolution that would far surpass the limits of the automotive sector. He decided to divide complex processes into small, easily repeatable tasks and assigning these to his workers separately instead of having everyone accompany a car along the entire production cycle. For one, this change reduced the time required to manufacture a car from 12 hours to 2,5 hours, significantly boosting output. But it also laid the foundation for employing robots in production.

It took until 1961, though, for the first manufacturing robot, the Unimate, to take up work in a New Jersey General Motors plant, sequencing and stacking hot metal plates. It resulted out of a discussion between George Devol and Joseph Engelberger at a cocktail party. They wondered why workers were still manually putting and taking objects, repetitive processes making for roughly 50% of tasks inside a factory. A robot, they argued, would be able to take over this work much more efficiently, around the clock, without tiring.

The following decades saw the rapid advancement of robotics technology, witnessing breakthroughs like the 6-axis robotic arm and the build-up of highly automated assembly lines in automotive and other plants — with development mostly driven by evolving demands in the automotive sector. However, this also meant more and more workers were displaced from their old jobs, freed up to trade in their blue-collar occupations in favor of white-collar jobs.

White collar jobs entailed higher purchasing power, which led to more selective and demanding buying behavior. To accommodate customers’ hunger for the new and exciting, production lines had to become more flexible to enable mass individualization. Hence at the end of the twentieth century, Cobots, robots designed to interact with humans in shared workspaces, were introduced. And with GM being one of the major sponsors of this advancement, once again, the automotive industry was among the key patrons of robotics development.

The first era of robotics development was hence driven by hardware innovation, a quest for efficiency, increased production frequency and automated processes. In the 21st century, innovation in industrial robotics transferred mostly to the software and control layer, while hardware is being refined further and evolving.

Robotics — beyond mobility

Looking beyond the industrial context and considering the breakthroughs in robotics development in other sectors, we are sure that what lies ahead of us is mainly powered by software and algorithms.

Already today, there are hotels that are entirely run by robot staff. There is robot dogs, like Boston Dynamics’ Spot, meant to carry heavy weight while hiking, measuring surroundings and watching over facilities. Then you can buy actual pet dogs, like Sony’s AIBO that almost behaves like a real dog, cuddling and showing emotions. Some would probably much rather call him a trojan horse, collecting data inside people’s homes.

Taking things to the extreme is the humanoid Sophia the Robot, the first robot to hold a citizenship, which was awarded to her by Saudi Arabia in 2017. With each of these advances, robots and artificial intelligence conquer a larger share of our lives, taking over surgeries and other medical decision-making, conducting vehicles and drones, and countless other tasks.

We believe while the first wave of robotic development was indeed driven by the automotive industry, the next evolutionary steps in robotics will come out of development in software and algorithms. In the words of Marc Andreesen, “software is eating the world”, and the robotics industry with it.

The Robotics Landscape

To develop a vision of what is to come in the next years for robotics in mobility, we took a look at the status quo regarding robotics startups in mobility and industrial applications. For this, we at Beyond1435 did a deep dive into robotics, focusing mostly on industrial use cases and discarding all things autonomous driving. The approach we took was mostly deductive by classifying the most innovative ventures in the space. Also, as Beyond1435’s scope is Europe and Israel, US and Asian companies were mostly excluded from the process. All in all, we identified the categories Hardware, Software and Full Stack.

Hardware

Components: In terms of hardware, there are companies working on single components of robots, e.g. 3D vision camera systems or other sensors improving the way robots can capture and process information about their surroundings, but also new and innovative grippers and other hardware components which improve the material handling capabilities. Here, one example is Righthand Robotics which combines vacuum technology with a traditional “3-finger gripper” to improve flexibility and allow one gripper to handle a greater variety of objects without adjustment and complicated programming.

Robots: The “robots” category can be further divided into those aimed at industrial use cases and others for service applications. In industrial robotics, a lot of innovation can be found when it comes to warehousing, with companies especially improving pick and place processes, but also developing new kinds of AGVs and goods-to-person applications, increasing efficiency in warehouses and order fulfillment.

Software

Although there is a lot going on in the hardware category, software is “where the magic happens”. Advances in software have the potential to unlock new applications or make existing ones more flexible and intuitive, delivering on the demands from manufacturers and enabling them to deliver on customer expectations for individualized mass production. Innovation can be clustered in three categories.

Operating Systems: On the foundational level, there are advances in robot operating systems. New, improved OS allow to run a diverse set of robots of multiple brands on the same software, making for better interoperability and decoupling the hardware purchasing decisions from software considerations. Examples are Cambrian Intelligence and mov.ai. Other startups focus on retrofitting existing hardware to make it “smarter”. Balyo, for example, is working on software to automate forklifts in warehouses.

Programming Suites: The second software layer approaches the topic from a different angle, improving and simplifying the programming of robots for specific tasks. Here, advances mainly revolve around developing suites that enable no-code programming of robots, either through offering a range of building blocks, each representing a movement or task, which can then be combined to define a process. This can be compared to libraries of functions and methods for coding languages like Python which hide the actual complexity from the end user. Another approach is that of enabling robots to copy and remember movements. Micropsi, for example, developed an application where a worker can guide the robotic arm through a series of movements which are recorded by the software and then put on “replay”, enabling the robot to do the movement on its own.

Specific Applications: The third sub-category is labeled specific applications. Here, navigation plays an important role, together with other applications in various areas.

Full Stack

The last building block of the robotics landscape is “Full Stack” technology. Here, we fitted in startups that provide both the robots and the software for one or more applications. A prominent example is Fabric, formerly known as Commonsense Robotics, which automate the operation of entire warehouses, even offering micro fulfillment centers as a service to interested customers. Others, like Franka Emika, focus more on combining their own robot with the respective programming suite, both optimized for each other.

What we currently see is the growing importance of improvements in software, both for specific applications and on a more general level, regarding operating systems and programming suites. Here, advances in artificial intelligence and machine learning have a great influence, enabling the development of more intuitive software, simplifying the use of robots and giving people without deep technological training and skills access to working with robots.

We predict the trend towards software as key driver of innovation to continue, by far outgrowing hardware development. This is even more evident when we look beyond the industrial context and consider the breakthroughs in robotics development in other applications. What is ahead of us is mainly powered by new software and algorithms. The hardware itself is but an enabler, giving a physical form to the software.
